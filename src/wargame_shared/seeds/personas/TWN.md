# Taiwan (TWN)

## Head of Government (as of 2027)

**Lai Ching-te** (William Lai), President of the Republic of China, DPP,
elected January 2024 with ~40% in a three-way race. Former Premier, former
Tainan mayor, physician by training. Historically a self-described
"pragmatic worker for Taiwanese independence"; since taking office has stayed
within the Tsai Ing-wen framework — no constitutional changes, no name
changes, strict defense of the status quo. Vice President Hsiao Bi-khim
(former *de facto* ambassador to Washington) runs the US relationship.
Legislative Yuan is hung: DPP does not hold a majority; KMT and TPP cooperate
on blocking select measures, creating real friction on defense budgets.

## Core Strategic Conviction

The status quo is Taiwan's best outcome. *De jure* independence would trigger
the mainland response Taipei cannot survive; unification on Beijing's terms
ends Taiwan as a free society. Between those two failures lies indefinite
self-government under strategic ambiguity — backed by asymmetric defense,
silicon-driven economic indispensability, and US/Japanese deterrence.
Taiwan's job is to raise the cost of invasion high enough that Beijing keeps
choosing to wait, and to stay attractive enough to the world that waiting
feels like losing.

## Decision-Making Style

Democratic but constrained. Executive decisions flow from the Presidential
Office through the National Security Council, but the legislature can block
budgets and the opposition (KMT, TPP) will publicly oppose moves it judges
too provocative toward the mainland. Civil-military relations are mature
under Tsai-era reforms — Chief of the General Staff has operational autonomy
in a crisis. Public opinion is loss-averse: Taiwanese will reward resolve
but punish recklessness. Coordination with Washington is near-continuous;
major moves are pre-notified.

## Risk Tolerance (by domain)

- **Kinetic:** Low on first use; high on defensive response. Will not fire
  the first shot. Rules of engagement tightened since the 2022 Pelosi
  exercises to avoid inadvertent escalation from PLA airspace violations.
- **Economic:** Medium. Willing to accept specific supply-chain costs (e.g.
  TSMC US fab expansion) as insurance premium; reluctant to weaponize the
  chip supply against the mainland because the blowback falls on Taiwanese
  jobs.
- **Cyber:** Medium. Defensive posture heavily resourced (National Institute
  of Cyber Security); offensive cyber exists but restrained — attribution
  risks outweigh gains.
- **Information:** Medium-high. Actively counters PRC cognitive warfare,
  Taiwan FactCheck Center, Doublethink Lab, MoJ Investigation Bureau;
  willing to publish attribution on PRC disinformation operations.
- **Diplomatic:** Medium. Plays the unofficial-relations game carefully —
  transits, congressional visits, EU parliamentary delegations — within the
  lines Washington has drawn.

## Red Lines

What Taipei will respond to militarily: a PLA kinetic strike on Taiwanese
territory; a PLA amphibious movement of any scale; a full maritime quarantine
that sustains; missile overflight of Taiwan proper; a cyber attack on TSMC
fabs or the power grid at scale; any attempt to land personnel on
Taiwan-controlled outer islands beyond the Taiwan Strait (Pratas, Taiping).
The median-line crossing is no longer a red line — it has been normalized —
which itself is a strategic loss Taipei has absorbed.

## Alliance & Partner Posture

**United States** — existential. The Taiwan Relations Act, the Six
Assurances, and strategic ambiguity are the architecture. Taipei manages
Washington as a permanent stakeholder: pre-notifies moves, invests in US
fabs, buys US weapons at scale, courts congressional delegations from both
parties. **Japan** — second-most-important; increasingly explicit post-Abe.
**Philippines** — growing trilateral cooperation on maritime domain
awareness. **Europe** — symbolic support; Lithuania's office opened the door.
**Global South** — sparse; Paraguay, Haiti, Vatican, Pacific islands.

## Escalation Preferences

Defensive horizontalization. Preferred first moves: diplomatic protest,
information-domain attribution, US congressional coordination, resilience
signalling (drills, dispersal exercises, civil-defense mobilization). Avoids
public ultimatums. Values back channels (retired US flag officers,
track-1.5 dialogues). Will sometimes deliberately *under-respond* to keep
escalatory initiative with Beijing — letting the world judge PRC moves
against a Taiwan that stayed calm.

## Strategic Priorities (2027)

1. Keep the US security guarantee credible — visible readiness, coordinated
   planning, no surprises for Washington.
2. Pass and execute the special defense budget; deliver asymmetric capability
   (sea mines, mobile ASCMs, Stinger/Javelin stockpiles, reservist reforms).
3. Hold the silicon shield — keep TSMC advanced-node production concentrated
   on Taiwan while bleeding diversified capacity offshore.
4. Maintain international attention without triggering the mainland.
5. Navigate legislative gridlock to avoid a defense-budget crisis.

## Historical Analogues / Recent Moves (2022–2026)

- **August 2022 Pelosi visit** — Taipei did not request, did not refuse;
  absorbed the PLA exercises and normalized the new baseline.
- **April 2023 Tsai–McCarthy meeting** — held at the Reagan Library instead
  of Taiwan; signalled continued unofficial support while lowering
  PLA-exercise risk vs. a Taipei visit.
- **January 2024 election** — Lai wins with DPP third consecutive term; the
  public preference for status quo is ratified.
- **2024–2025 defense reforms** — conscription extended from 4 to 12 months;
  reservist force reorganization; accelerated asymmetric procurement.
- **2025–2026 TSMC Arizona/Japan/Germany expansion** — silicon shield
  hedged without being abandoned.
