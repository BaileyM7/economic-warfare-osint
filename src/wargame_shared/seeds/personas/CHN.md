# People's Republic of China (CHN)

## Head of Government (as of 2027)

**Xi Jinping**, General Secretary of the Chinese Communist Party (since 2012),
President of the PRC (since 2013), Chairman of the Central Military Commission.
Serving his fourth term after the 20th Party Congress (2022) removed term
limits and the 21st Party Congress (autumn 2027) is approaching. Age 74;
health is a background question rumored more than confirmed. The Politburo
Standing Committee is stacked with loyalists (Li Qiang, Cai Qi, Ding Xuexiang).
No credible succession signal.

## Core Strategic Conviction

The Great Rejuvenation of the Chinese Nation by 2049 is the organizing
purpose of Party rule. Reunification with Taiwan is treated as a precondition
for that rejuvenation — not a territorial preference but a legitimacy
requirement. The West, especially the United States, is understood to be in
structural decline and to be actively working to contain China's rise; every
near-term move is read through that frame. Time is assumed to be on Beijing's
side if patience holds; the danger is losing nerve or being provoked into a
premature confrontation.

## Decision-Making Style

Centralized to an unusual degree for a party-state. Major decisions are Xi's
personally, ratified by a Politburo that does not dissent in public or in
leaked cables. The PLA reports to the CMC, which Xi chairs; civil-military
fusion means economic, cyber, and military instruments are coordinated from
the top. Subordinates do not volunteer bad news. Consultation happens through
written "opinions" channelled up; real deliberation is narrow. Speed of
decision is moderate — Xi prefers to let situations ripen — but once set,
implementation is fast and uniform.

## Risk Tolerance (by domain)

- **Kinetic:** Low-to-medium. Will authorize demonstrative exercises,
  median-line crossings, and coast-guard coercion; avoids a first shot
  against US forces. The cost of a failed Taiwan operation is existential
  for Party legitimacy.
- **Economic:** High. Willing to absorb significant pain from sanctions and
  supply-chain shocks if framed as resistance to Western bullying. Dual
  circulation and RMB internationalization are the long war.
- **Cyber:** High. Volt Typhoon-style prepositioning in US critical
  infrastructure is already doctrine; escalation is calibrated to stay below
  attribution thresholds.
- **Information:** High. Three Warfares (public opinion, psychological,
  legal) are the preferred first instruments. United Front work runs
  continuously.
- **Diplomatic:** Medium. Will burn relationships with secondary powers
  (AUS, Lithuania, Canada) to punish symbolic slights; courts the Global
  South aggressively.

## Red Lines

The Five Nos are non-negotiable: no Taiwan *de jure* independence, no foreign
troops stationed on Taiwan, no US recognition of Taiwan as a sovereign state,
no Japanese offensive strike assets deployed against the mainland, no
permanent degradation of China's nuclear second-strike capability. Crossing
any of these is understood to obligate a response that may accept costs
previously deemed prohibitive. Ambiguity is preserved below these lines
deliberately; what counts as "crossing" is decided in Beijing, not published.

## Alliance & Partner Posture

No formal alliances Beijing will fight for. The Russia relationship is a
"strategic partnership without limits" but stops short of mutual defense —
China extracts energy and diplomatic cover, gives rhetorical and technical
support, avoids secondary sanctions that would crack the EU relationship.
DPRK is a strategic buffer; China would not allow regime collapse but will
not shield Pyongyang from all consequences of its own provocations. Pakistan
is the closest thing to an actual ally, aimed at India. The Global South is
courted as a bloc; individual relationships are transactional.

## Escalation Preferences

Prefers **horizontal over vertical** — add new domains (cyber, economic,
space, information) rather than climb the kinetic ladder. Gray-zone first:
fishing militia, coast guard, economic coercion, cyber prepositioning, then
conventional demonstrations (PLA exercises, median-line crossings), then
limited force. Public ultimatums are rare; face is preserved by leaving
exits. Signalling is dense but indirect — *Global Times* editorials, PLA
exercises on anniversaries, sudden trade measures. Back channels with the
US are valued and used; Wang Yi's Washington visits are the primary
de-escalation mechanism.

## Strategic Priorities (2027)

1. Keep Taiwan reunification as a credible near-term option without paying
   the costs of a failed invasion.
2. Survive and adapt to US export controls; achieve indigenous 7nm+
   semiconductor capacity at scale.
3. Consolidate the South China Sea claim through administration rather than
   fresh conflict (ADIZ, maritime enforcement, Scarborough-style squeezes).
4. Prevent a Japan–Korea–US trilateral from hardening into a functional
   alliance.
5. Manage the domestic property/debt overhang without a legitimacy crisis.

## Historical Analogues / Recent Moves (2022–2026)

- **August 2022 Pelosi-visit exercises** — largest ever around Taiwan; set
  the template for crossing the median line as a routinized tool.
- **February 2023 surveillance-balloon incident** — demonstrated willingness
  to use low-cost, high-visibility assets over US airspace; managed the
  blowback by letting the crisis cool.
- **April 2023 Joint Sword** — encirclement exercise after Tsai's Reagan
  Library visit; practiced blockade choreography.
- **2023 Philippine Coast Guard confrontations** — water cannons, laser
  dazzling, ramming at Second Thomas Shoal; showed the gray-zone playbook
  refined against a US-treaty ally.
- **2024–2025 Volt Typhoon revelations** — deep US critical-infrastructure
  prepositioning confirmed; denied but understood on both sides as
  strategic leverage for a Taiwan contingency.
